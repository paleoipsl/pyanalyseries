Now that you are ready to use PyAnalySeries, an important point is how to work with your data, 
in other words, how to import series.
<break time="1s"/>

Many scientists working with field or instrumental data rely on spreadsheet formats.
<break time="1s"/>

For this reason, PyAnalySeries allows you to directly copy and paste data from your spreadsheet 
into the import window.
<break time="1s"/>

Let’s look at this in more detail.
<break time="3s"/>

Let’s open a new worksheet to host our data.
<break time="3s"/>

Rename the file if needed.
<break time="6s"/>

Data can be imported in X, Y format, or with multiple series such as X, Y1, Y2, Y3.
From your spreadsheet, select the columns and press Control C.
<break time="2s"/>

Then, in the import window available from the Create menu, press Control V.
<break time="10s"/>

There is no need to clear the import window; simply paste your data directly into it.
<break time="1s"/>

Columns can also be reorganized once pasted.
If the columns are not ordered as X, Y1, Y2, you can rearrange them as needed.
<break time="6s"/>

Click and hold the column title, then drag it to the desired position.
<break time="5s"/>

It is also possible to import a set of pointers in X, Y format.
<break time="10s"/>

Missing Y values can be preserved, allowing you to keep the data exactly as it was originally produced.
<break time="1s"/>

Let’s now examine the effect of this checkbox on the imported series.
<break time="15s"/>

Import is independent of the decimal separator, whether comma or dot.
Values are internally standardized using the dot.
<break time="1s"/>

Column titles can include LaTeX-style characters, which will be correctly rendered in the plots.
<break time="1s"/>

You may also want to reorganize items internally.
Just drag them to the desired order.
<break time="4s"/>

Do not forget to save your worksheet with your imported data with a Ctrl S.
<break time="4s"/>
