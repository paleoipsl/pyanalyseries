The application can also provide temporal variations of astronomical parameters and insolation series.

To do so, let’s choose 'Create' and then 'Insolation / Astronomical series'.
<break time="1s" />

In the Type field, we can choose between various astronomical parameters and different types of insolation.
We can then select the astronomical solution to use, with the reference shown on the right-hand side.
<break time="2s" />

We select the time period over which the series is computed.
Past ages can be entered as negative or positive values.

We can choose the time unit: years or thousands of years, noted k y r in the interface.
<break time="1s" />

First, we plot the daily insolation using the 'Laskar 2004' astronomical solution.
To represent Northern Hemisphere summer solstice insolation at 65°North, we enter 65 in the latitude field and 90 in the true longitude field.
<break time="2s" />

We can, for example, plot this insolation over the last 160 thousand years, with a step of 0.2.

Please note that the application relies on true longitudes and not calendar dates. 
The true longitude is illustrated in the schematic on the right-hand side: it corresponds to the angle 'lambda' between the vernal point 'gamma', defined as 0°, and the position of the Sun as seen from Earth at a given time.
<break time="1s" />

The reason for using true longitudes instead of dates is illustrated in the second figure, accessible using the carousel navigation arrows: the length of the seasons, defined by fixed true-longitude intervals, varies through time. This is ignored when using fixed dates such as June 21.
<break time="1s" />

In our case study, we also plot the daily insolation at a latitude of 5°South for the Southern Hemisphere summer solstice, still over the last 160 thousand years.

We then enter -5 in the latitude field and 270 in the true longitude field. 
<break time="2s" />

We can click on 'Import series' to save the insolation time series.
<break time="1s" />

Finally, we calculate and plot the integrated insolation between two true longitudes, selected in the Type field.
<break time="1s" />

We plot the insolation integrated over the Southern Hemisphere summer period, namely December, January, and February.
<break time="1s" />

We need to determine the corresponding true longitude values.

We know that the true longitude of the Southern Hemisphere summer solstice is 270°.

We assume that 360 days correspond to 360° of longitude.
The Southern Hemisphere summer period, from early December to late February, roughly corresponds to true longitudes between 250 and 340.
We enter these values in True Longitude 1 and True Longitude 2.
<break time="2s" />

We click on 'Import series' to save this Southern Hemisphere integrated summer insolation.
Take a moment to name the series.
<break time="4s" />

We can now compare our filtered 'PercAndes' record with this Southern Hemisphere integrated summer insolation curve.
<break time="2s" />

We click on 'Display Together' and use a separate vertical axis.
<break time="2s" />

We observe that high values of the filtered 'PercAndes' record generally correspond to high insolation values.
<break time="1s" />

The scientific interpretation is that Southern Hemisphere integrated summer insolation controls, on precessional time scales, the intensity of the South American summer monsoon system, and thus the delivery of Andean-derived terrigenous material at the core site.
