Let’s explore the interpolation feature, a central component of PyAnalySeries ! 
Thanks to the application’s interactivity, defining an interpolation becomes a simple and intuitive process !

Let’s consider a real use case. 
The example files are available in the installation directory.

First, open the files. 
You can load multiple files, referred to as WorkSheets in the application.
The format is based on the open .xlsx standard.

Open the two files as shown in the video.
<break time="1s" />

Now select the variables to interpolate, along with their reference series — here, delta C 13 and delta O 18.
Use the Control key to select multiple series.
The selection order does not matter, but for clarity, we will choose the reference first.
<break time="2s" />

Open the interpolation window. 
In practice, this consists of identifying correspondences between two series.

This is where interactivity becomes essential.
You can place tie points either on transitions or directly on data points.
<break time="2s" />

We now have four series selected.
<break time="1s" />

Start with delta O 18.
Define the reference and the series to interpolate using the selection controls.
<break time="2s" />

Identify similar transition zones.
<break time="2s" />

Hold Shift and click to place the vertical dashed line in each window.
Then press C to connect them.
<break time="1s" />

This creates a pair of corresponding points — called pointers.
<break time="1s" />

Select another transition and repeat the operation.
<break time="2s" />

Once two pairs are defined, the interpolation appears automatically.
Press Z, or enable “Show interpolated curve”, to toggle its display.
<break time="2s" />

You can also compare interpolation methods — linear or polynomial — and observe their effect.
<break time="2s" />

Now refine the interpolation by placing a pointer directly on a data point.
Hold Control and click on the point.
<break time="2s" />

Repeat this on the reference series, then connect the two pointers.
<break time="2s" />

In short:
Shift-click places a free pointer.
Control-click snaps to a data point.
<break time="2s" />

To undo the last connection, click “Remove last connection” or press U.
<break time="1s" />

To delete a specific connection, hover over it — it turns red — then press X.
<break time="1s" />

Press uppercase X to remove all pointers.
<break time="2s" />

You can review all pointer values in the “Pointers” tab.
<break time="1s" />

In the “Pointers plot” tab, you can visualize X2 as a function of X1, along with the gradient.
If X2 is time and X1 is depth, this gradient represents the sedimentation rate.
<break time="2s" />

During the process, you may need to switch variables to refine the alignment.
<break time="1s" />

Switch to delta C 13.
Define a new correspondence and connect the points.
<break time="2s" />

Then switch back to delta O 18.
<break time="2s" />

This iterative workflow allows you to progressively improve the interpolation.
<break time="2s" />

Let’s now look at the result produced by an experienced user.
<break time="4s" />

Once satisfied, you can save either the interpolation or the interpolation and the current interpolated series.
<break time="2s" />

You can also load an existing pointer file to refine or adjust your interpolation.
<break time="2s" />

In the next step, we will see how to apply this interpolation to a set of series.
