PyAnalySeries provides several methods
to estimate the power spectral density of a signal.
<break time="1s"/>

These methods rely on the Pyleoclim module,
which itself builds on spectral analysis methods available in SciPy.
<break time="1s"/>

The goal here is to make these methods easily accessible
within the application, so they can be tested and compared interactively.
<break time="1s"/>

For each method, the most essential parameters
have been exposed in the interface,
allowing quick exploration of the different spectral estimators.
<break time="1s"/>

Let’s return to our example.
<break time="1s"/>

We use the “PercAndes” series
to investigate its significant periodicities,
using the different methods on the original, resampled, or smoothed series.
<break time="1s"/>

Select a series, then go to Process
and choose “Spectral Estimation”,
with PSD standing for Power Spectral Density.
<break time="1s"/>

The Periodogram is the most direct estimator
of the power spectral density.
It is simple and easy to interpret,
but it can be noisy.
<break time="2s"/>

The idea is to quickly explore the impact of different parameters.
<break time="1s"/>

Press Compute, or simply modify a parameter,
and the plot updates automatically.
<break time="1s"/>

You can clean up your tests
by removing the last spectrum from the stack
using the “Remove last” option.
<break time="1s"/>

Welch’s method improves the estimate
by splitting the signal into overlapping segments
and averaging their spectra.
<break time="2s"/>

MTM, for Multi-Taper Method,
uses several tapers to obtain a more stable spectral estimate.
<break time="2s"/>

Lomb–Scargle is especially useful
for unevenly spaced time series.
<break time="2s"/>

Wavelet analysis, using WWZ,
explores how periodicities evolve through time.
<break time="1s"/>

For each method, you can access the corresponding documentation
through a dedicated link.
<break time="3s"/>

Each method also has its own prerequisites,
such as standardization or interpolation.
<break time="3s"/>

These steps are handled automatically by the interface,
so there is no need to perform them beforehand.
<break time="1s"/>

The spectrum is displayed on a logarithmic scale
and fully supports zooming.
<break time="1s"/>

Once saved, each computed spectrum appears in the main window
with its dedicated icon.
<break time="6s"/>

Using “Display Together” allows you
to visualize them simultaneously.
<break time="6s"/>

In practice, we compute and save
the MTM and wavelet spectra on the resampled record.
<break time="2s"/>

For advanced spectral analysis, it is recommended to explore Pyleoclim
in a Python or notebook environment,
to fine-tune method-specific parameters
and ensure full reproducibility of the results.
<break time="2s"/>
